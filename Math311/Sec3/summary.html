# Summary - Section 3 ## [Basics](Fields.html) ### [Cross Product](Fields.html#cross-product) The cross product of two vectors is defined as: $$ (a_1, a_2, a_3) \times (b_1, b_2, b_3) = \begin{vmatrix} \hat i && \hat j && \hat k \\ a_1 && a_2 && a_3 \\ b_1 && b_2 && b_3 \\ \end{vmatrix} $$ $$ = (a_2b_3 - b_2a_3)\hat i - (a_1b_3 - b_1a_3)\hat j + (b_1a_2 - a_1b_2)\hat k $$ ### Scalar Fields Scalar fields are function which take in a point and produce a value: $$w = f(x,y,z)$$ ### [Vector Fields](Fields.html#vector-fields) Vector fields are functions which take in a point and produce a vector: $$\vec x = F(x,y,z)$$ ### [Del Operator](Fields.html#derivatives) The $\nabla$ operator is similar to differentiation for scalar and vector fields: $$\nabla = \left({\partial \over \partial x}, {\partial \over \partial y}, {\partial \over \partial z}\right)$$ #### Gradient $$\nabla f = f\left({\partial \over \partial x}, {\partial \over \partial y}, {\partial \over \partial z}\right) = \left({\partial f \over \partial x}, {\partial f \over \partial y}, {\partial f \over \partial z}\right)$$ #### Curl $$ \nabla \times \vec F = \begin{vmatrix} \hat i && \hat j && \hat k \\ {\partial \over \partial x} && {\partial \over \partial y} && {\partial \over \partial z} \\ F_1 && F_2 && F_3 \\ \end{vmatrix} $$ #### Divergence $$\nabla \cdot \vec F = {\partial F_1 \over \partial x} + {\partial F_2 \over \partial y} + {\partial F_3 \over \partial z}$$ Notably, $div(curl(\vec F)) = 0$. ## [Line Integrals](Vector_Func.html) Line integrals are the integral along a path. They are calculated by plugging in the parameterization for the path into the function, and taking the length for $ds$. Essentially, you just need to convert the standard formula into an integral of $t$. Both line and surface integrals require a parameterization of the line or surface before it can be integrated over. ### [Scalar Line Integrals](Vector_Func.html#scalar-line-integral) $$\int\limits_{\vec x} fds = \int_a^b f(\vec x) ||\vec x'||dt$$ ### [Vector Line Integrals](Vector_Func.html#vector-line-integrals) $$\int\limits_{\vec x} \vec F \cdot d\vec s = \int_a^b \vec F(\vec x) \cdot (\vec x') dt$$ ### [Green's Theorem](Line_Int.html) Green's Theorem connects path integrals to double integrals (of an area) in two dimensions. It states: $$\oint\limits_{\vec x} \vec F\cdot d\vec s = \iint\limits_{D} \left({\partial F_1 \over \partial x} - {\partial F_2 \over \partial y}\right)dy dx$$ If $\left({\partial F_1 \over \partial x} - {\partial F_2 \over \partial y}\right) = 1$, then it becomes an area integral (results in the area of $D$). ## [Surface Integrals](Surface_Int.html) Surface integrals are similar to path integrals, except instead of a parameterization of $t$, it is a parameterization of both $t$ and $s$ that you are integrating over. Instead of path length, it uses normals and area. So, you need to convert the integral into a double integral of $s$ and $t$. ### [Normals](Surface_Int.html#surface-area) The normal is defined as: $$\vec N = \vec T_s \times \vec T_t = {\partial \vec S \over \partial s} \times {\partial \vec S \over \partial t}$$ ### [Scalar Surface Integrals](Surface_Int.html#scalar-surface-integral) $$\iint\limits_{\vec S} fdS = \iint_{s_0, t_0}^{s_1, t_1} f(\vec S) ||\vec N||ds dt$$ ### [Vector Surface Integrals](Surface_Int.html#vector-surface-integral) $$\iint\limits_{\vec S} \vec F \cdot d\vec S = \iint_{s_0, t_0}^{s_1, t_1} F(\vec S)\cdot \vec N ds dt$$ ### [Stokes' Theorem](Stokes.html#stokes'-theorem) Stokes' Theorem connects surface integrals to line integrals. It states that a surface integral of the curl of a vector field is the same as the integral around its boundary, with the surface and path oriented by the right-hand-rule. $$\iint\limits_{\vec S}(\nabla \times \vec F) \cdot d\vec S = \oint\limits_{\partial S} \vec F \cdot d\vec s$$ ### [Gauss's Theorem](Stokes.html#gauss's-theorem) Gauss's Theorem connects surface integrals to three dimensional integrals (volume integrals). It states that the integral of a vector field over a closed surface (oriented outward) is the same as the triple integral of the divergence of the field over the volume it encloses. $$\oint\limits_{\partial V} \vec F \cdot d\vec S = \iiint\limits_{\vec V} \nabla \cdot \vec F dx dy dz$$ If $\nabla \cdot \vec F = 1$, then this becomes just the volume of $V$.
