# Practice Problems What follows is a summarization and paraphrasing of Dr. Nguyen's review lecture. For a more detailed summary, see [this page](summary.html). ## First, a brief summary It is recommended to review the HW problems, as well as lecture notes (specifically examples and notes) and any practice problems available in the online classroom. ## Chapter 1 - Linear Systems and Matrices The majority of computation in problems relies on using the three basic operations to transform the matrix to RE form, where lead and free variables can be identified, and then reduce it more to reduced RE form or solve through bask substitution. The matrix operations were also defined, including $\pm$, scalar multiplication, matrix multiplication, and transpose. There were also many algebra rules introduced for matrices, but most notably that $AB \ne BA$. Finally, matrix inverse, and the form of solving it through an augmented matrix ($[A|I] \to [I|A^{-1}]$). Non-singular matrices will have an inverse, meaning $Ax=b$ has a unique solution $x = A^{-1}b$ (note the order of multiplication here). ## Chapter 2 - Determinant Here, the method to compute the determinant was shown, where we expand on one row or one column. Determinant properties were mentioned, for example the easy matrices for determinants and the effects of elementary operations, as well as algebraic rules for it. The most important rule is that $det(A) \ne 0$ if the matrix $A$ is non-singular. ## Chapter 3 - Vector Spaces Here we get into a more abstract setting. First, we defined a vector space: it has addition and scalar multiplication, and they satisfy 8 axioms. You will need to know how to prove/disprove if a space is a vector space. In addition, subspaces were defined: they are a subset of a vector space which is closed under addition and scalar multiplication. Linear independence was also introduced, defined as when a linear combination of the vectors cannot be 0 unless all coefficients are 0. A way to test this was shown where if the determinant is non-zero, the vectors are independent. Basis was defined as a spanning set which is independent, and dimension as the number of vectors in a basis. Three different situations for changing basis were introduced, where a transition matrix transforms vectors from one space to another. Row, column, and null spaces were introduced, where the $Rank(A)$ is the dimension of the row or column space, or the number of lead variables, and $Nullity(A)$ is the number of free variables or the dimension of the null space. The $Rank + Nullity =$ the number of variables (columns in the matrix). > Note: to disprove something, you can simply use a counter-example. ## Practice Problems/Examples ### True of False > Note: true or false will not require an explanation; the other questions will (they should ask you to explain or give reasoning). 1.
