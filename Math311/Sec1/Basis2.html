# Basis and Rank ## Change of Basis Given some basis of $V$, and vector $v \in V$ can be written uniquely as $$v = a_1v_1 + a_2 v_2 + ... +a_nv_n$$ > Definition: Coordinates > We call $a_1 ... a_n$ the **coordinates** of $v$ relative to this basis. Here we can see one of the reasons or usefulness of bases: they can be used to represent coordinates of $V$, such as how points can be written as $x, y, z$, using the basis $\left\{ \left(\begin{array}{c}1 \\ 0 \\ 0\end{array}\right), \left(\begin{array}{c}0 \\ 1 \\ 0\end{array}\right),\left(\begin{array}{c}0 \\ 0 \\ 1\end{array}\right) \right\}$. ~~Example 1 Using the standard basis of $\mathbb{R^2}$: $$\left\{ \left(\begin{array}{c}1 \\ 0 \end{array}\right), \left(\begin{array}{c}0 \\ 1 \end{array}\right) \right\}$$ $$\begin{array}{c} e_1 \end{array}\begin{array}{c} e_2 \end{array}$$ Any vector can be written in coordinates as a linear combination of $e_1, e_2$. Ex: $\left(\begin{array}{c}2 \\ 7 \end{array}\right)$ can be written as $2,7$ relative to the basis $e_1, e_2$ ~~ If we change the basis, the coordinates of the vector will change. To change basis from $(e_1, e_2)$ (the standard basis) to $(u_1, u_2)$ (the new basis), we multiply the original coordinates by the inverse of $U = [u_1\ u_2]$ $$v_{other\ basis} = U^{-1}v_{standard}$$ ~~an example changing from standard to new $\left\{ \left(\begin{array}{c}1 \\ 0 \end{array}\right), \left(\begin{array}{c}1 \\ 1 \end{array}\right) \right\}$ form a basis of $\mathbb{R}^2$. Find coordinates of $\left(\begin{array}{c}2 \\ 7 \end{array}\right)$ relative to these vectors $u_1, u_2$. Here, we solve the system: $$[u_1 u_2]\left[\begin{array}{c}\alpha_1 \\ \alpha_2 \end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{c}2 \\ 7 \end{array}\right]$$ So, we find the inverse of $U = [u_1\ u_2]$: $$ [U|I] = \left[\begin{array}{cc|cc}1 & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right] $$ $$ [I|U^{-1}] = \left[\begin{array}{cc|cc}1 & 0 & 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right] $$ And we multiply by it to find $\left[\begin{array}{c}\alpha_1 \\ \alpha_2 \end{array}\right]$: $$ \left[\begin{array}{c}\alpha_1 \\ \alpha_2 \end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 1\end{array}\right] \left[\begin{array}{c}2 \\ 7 \end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{c}5 \\ 7 \end{array}\right] $$ ~~ If we start from $(v_1, v_2)$ (any basis) to $(e_1, e_2)$ (the standard basis), the coordinates in the standard basis will be the original coordinates multiplied by the matrix $V = [v_1\ v_2]$. $$v_{standard} = Vv_{other\ basis}$$ ~~an example from any basis to standard $\left\{ \left(\begin{array}{c}1 \\ 2 \end{array}\right), \left(\begin{array}{c}-1 \\ 1 \end{array}\right) \right\}$ form a basis. Find coordinates of $v$ relative to $e_1, e_2$ given $v = 3v_1 + 4v_2$. We have the system: $$ v = V \left[\begin{array}{c}3 \\ 4 \end{array}\right] $$ And substituting in: $$ v = \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & -1 \\ 2 & 1\end{array}\right] \left[\begin{array}{c}3 \\ 4 \end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{c}1 \\ 10 \end{array}\right] $$ So we can say: $$v = e_1 + 10e_2$$ ~~ In the general case, where we are changing from $(v_1, v_2)$ to $(u_1, u_2)$ (both general bases), change from $V$ to standard, and from standard to $U$. This will be the same as multiplying by $V$ and then by $U^{-1}$: $$v_{new} = U^{-1}Vv_{old}$$ > Theorem: > Given a basis $v_1 ... v_n$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$, a vector $v$ has coordinates $a_1 ... a_n$ relative to $v_1 ... v_n$. > With a new basis $u_1 ... u_n$, the coordinates of $v$ can be computed as: > $$U^{-1}V\left(\begin{array}{c}a_1 \\ ... \\ a_n \end{array}\right)$$ > Where $U = [u_1 ... u_n]$ and $V = [v_1 ... v_n]$ ~~an example for the general case $\left\{ \left(\begin{array}{c}1 \\ 3 \end{array}\right), \left(\begin{array}{c}2 \\ 1 \end{array}\right) \right\}$ form a basis. Find coordinates of $v$ relative to $u_1, u_2$ given $v = 5v_1 - 2v_2$ and the basis: $$U = \left\{ \left(\begin{array}{c}3 \\ 4 \end{array}\right), \left(\begin{array}{c}0 \\ 1 \end{array}\right) \right\}$$ First, we find the inverse of $U$, then plug into the formula: $$ U^{-1}V\left(\begin{array}{c}5 \\ -2 \end{array}\right) $$ $$ \left[\begin{array}{cc} 3 & 0 \\ 4 & 1\end{array}\right]^{-1} \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 1\end{array}\right] \left[\begin{array}{c}5 \\ -2 \end{array}\right] $$ ~~ ## Row, Column, and Null Spaces > Definition: > Given an $m \times n$ matrix $A$, > - The span of all row vectors is called the **row space** > - The span of all column vectors is called the **column space** > - The **null space** of A can be found by: > $$N(A) = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^n | Ax = \bar 0\}$$ How do we find a basis and dimension for row, column, and null spaces? Use elimination to get to RE form and from there we have the info we need. To find the basis, take the matrix which represents the spanning vectors and convert it to RE form. Then, eliminate the all-zero rows (keeping the non-zero rows *from RE form*). The remaining vectors will form a basis. ~~a row space example $$ \textbf{A = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & -2 & 3 \\ 2 & -5 & 1 \\ 1 & -4 & -7 \end{array} \right] $$ 1. The row space is: $\left\{ \left(\begin{array}{c}1 \\ -2 \\ 3\end{array}\right), \left(\begin{array}{c}2 \\ -5 \\ 1\end{array}\right),\left(\begin{array}{c}1 \\ -4 \\ -7\end{array}\right) \right\}$ To find a basis (no redundant vectors), we have to change it to RE form. $$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & -2 & 3 \\ 2 & -5 & 1 \\ 1 & -4 & -7 \end{array} \right] $$ $$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & -2 & 3 \\ 0 & -1 & -5 \\ 0 & -2 & -10 \end{array} \right] $$ $$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & -2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 5 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array} \right] $$ Since the last row is $0$, it is redundant, so it is not necessary in the basis. Let's remove it: $$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & -2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 5 \end{array} \right] $$ These rows form a basis of the row space of $A$, and $\dim(row\ space(A)) = 2$ ~~ To find the column space basis, take the columns from the original matrix which correspond with the lead variables in RE form. ~~a column space example Using the same matrix from earlier: $$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & -2 & 3 \\ 2 & -5 & 1 \\ 1 & -4 & -7 \end{array} \right] \to \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & -2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 5 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array} \right] $$ Note that we have two lead variables, $x_1$ and $x_2$, and one free variable: $x_3$. We can take the columns of the original matrix with lead variables to form the column space. $$ \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & -2 \\ 2 & -5 \\ 1 & -4 \end{array} \right] $$ So, the basis of the column space will be: $$ \left\{ \left(\begin{array}{c}1 \\ 2 \\ 1\end{array}\right), \left(\begin{array}{c}2 \\ -5 \\ 4\end{array}\right) \right\} $$ ~~ And for null space basis, simply solve the system $Ax = \bar 0$ using RE form. ~~a null space example Using the matrix from earlier: $$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & -2 & 3 \\ 2 & -5 & 1 \\ 1 & -4 & -7 \end{array} \right] \to \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & -2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 5 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array} \right] $$ The null space requires $Ax = \bar 0$, so we can solve for $x$ from here: $$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & -2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 5 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ x_1 & x_2 & x_3 \end{array} \right] $$ Solving gives: $$ \begin{cases} x_1 = -12\alpha \\ x_2 = -5\alpha \\ x_3 = \alpha \end{cases} $$ And the null space can be represented as: $$ x = \alpha \left(\begin{array}{c}-13 \\ -5 \\ 1\end{array}\right) $$ So the basis of the null space is $\left(\begin{array}{c}-13 \\ -5 \\ 1\end{array}\right)$, and the dimension is $1$. ~~ > Theorem: Rank-Nullity > Given an $m \times n$ matrix $A$: > - $$Rank(A) = \dim(row\ space(A)) = \dim(col\ space(A)) = \# lead\ variables$$ > - $$Nullity(A) = \dim(N(A)) = \# free\ variables$$ > - $$Rank(A) + Nullity(A) = \# variables = n$$ ~~Example 2 Given $A$ is a $5\times 7$ matrix, $b\in \mathbb{R}^5$: What are the possible # of solutions for $Ax = b$ if $Rank(A)=4$? From Chapter 1, we know there is either: 1. 1 solution 2. $\infty$ solutions 3. 0 solutions We know from the Rank that the last row of $A$ in RE form will be all $0$s. Therefore, if the last element of $b$ is not 0, there is no solution. $$ [A|b] = \left[ \begin{array}{cccc|c} ? & ? & ? & ? & b_1 \\ &&... \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & b_n \end{array} \right] $$ From the rank-nullity theorem, we know there are three free variables. So, if the last element is $0$, then there are an infinite number of solutions. ~~
