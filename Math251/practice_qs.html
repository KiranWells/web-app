Use Stokes' Theorem to find the surface integral of $\iint\limits_S curl\ \vec F \cdot dr$ where $\vec F = \langle \arctan(1-x^2) + 2x^2y, e^{\frac{1}{y}} - 2xy^2 \rangle$ and $S$ is the [surface][1] of the sphere with radius 1 above the $xy$-plane. First, notice that we are using the formula backwards, going from a surface integral to a line integral. This means we can change the problem to: $$\oint\limits_C \vec F \cdot dr$$ Next, let's define $C$. Since the surface is bordered by the plane z = 0, as you can see [here][1], we can define $C$ as the circle with radius 1. $$ C = \begin{cases} x = (1)\cos t \\ y = (1)\sin t \\ z = 0 \end{cases} $$ Now we need to notice that the curve is in the xy-plane, and that the field only has $x$ and $y$ portions. This means we can integrate in two dimensions, and since $C$ is closed, Green's Theorem applies. $$\int\limits_C \vec F \cdot dr = \iint\limits_D \left({\delta Q\over \delta x} - {\delta P\over \delta y}\right)dxdy$$ Now to plug $\vec F$ into the formula: $$\iint\limits_D \left({\delta (e^{\frac{1}{y}} - 2xy^2)\over \delta x} - {\delta (\arctan(1-x^2) + 2x^2y)\over \delta y}\right)dxdy$$ $$\iint\limits_D \left(- 2y^2 - 2x^2\right)dxdy$$ The domain $D$ in this case is the circle with radius $1$, so we can conveniently convert to a polar integral. $$\int\limits_0^{2\pi}\int\limits_0^1 (-2r^2)r\ drd\theta$$ $$\int\limits_0^{2\pi}d\theta * -\frac{1}{2}r^4 |_0^1$$ $$2\pi * -\frac{1}{2}$$ $$-\pi$$

Use the divergence theorem to find the surface integral of $S$, where $S$ is the [surface][2] of the volume bounded by the paraboloid $z = x^2 + y^2$ and the plane $z = 4$, where $\vec F = \langle x^3, \sin x + y^3, z^2+ xy\rangle$ $$\iint\limits_S \vec F\cdot dr = \iiint\limits_E div\ \vec FdV$$ So, let's first find $div\ \vec F$. $$= {\delta\over\delta x}(x^3) + {\delta\over\delta y}(\sin x + y^3) + {\delta\over\delta z}(z^2 + xy)$$ $$= 3x^2 + 3y^2 + 2z$$ And now determine the [volume][2] we are integrating over. Use Stokes' Theorem (sorry, last time) to find the curve integral $\oint\limits_C \vec F \cdot dr$, where $\vec F = \langle xy, x^2, 3z\rangle$ and $C$ is the [intersection][3] of the cylinder $9 = x^2 + y^2$ and the plane $2x + z = 1$ above the $xy$-plane. First, find $curl\ \vec F$: $$ curl\ \vec F = \begin{vmatrix} i && j && k \\ dx && dy && dz \\ P && Q && R \end{vmatrix} = (Rdy - Qdz)i + (Pdz - Rdx)j + (Qdx - Pdy)k $$ $$= (0 - 0)i + (0 - 0)j + (2x-x)k$$ Now, we need to parameterize $S$. Since it is the intersection of a plane and cylinder, we can just use the plane. To identify the $r_u \times r_v$, notice that conveniently for planes, it is equal to the normal. $$\langle 2,0,1 \rangle$$ And now we integrate: $$\iint\limits_D \langle 0,0,x \rangle \cdot \langle 2,0,1 \rangle dA$$ $$\iint\limits_D x dA$$ The domain of $S$ is just the area inside the cylinder, which is the circle from $x^2 + y^2 = 9$. We can make it more convenient by converting to polar coordinates: $$\int\limits_0^{2\pi}\int\limits_0^3 r\cos\theta r\ drd\theta$$ $$\int\limits_0^{2\pi} \cos\theta d\theta * 1/3r^3|_0^3$$ $$0 * 9$$ $$0$$

Find the line integral $\oint\limits_C \vec F \cdot dr$ where $\vec F = \langle ye^z, xe^z - ze^{yz}, xye^z - ye^{yz}\rangle$ and $C$ is the curve $\vec r(t) = \langle \sin(2t), \cos^2(t/2), 3t + 1 \rangle$ from $t = 0$ to $t = \pi$ This integral can be simplified by using the fundamental theorem: $$\int\limits_C \nabla f \cdot dr = f(B) - f(A)$$ So, we find a function $f$ such that $\nabla f = \vec F$. Start by finding the integral of the first section. $$\int ye^zdx = xye^z + h(y,z)$$ Now, we find the $h(y,z)$ that works for the others. With some intuition, it can be found to be: $$xye^z - e^yz$$ And now we plug in the values for the beginning and end of $C$: $$A = \langle \sin(0), cos^2(0), 0+1\rangle$$ $$B = \langle \sin(2\pi), cos^2(\pi/2), 3\pi+1\rangle$$ $$0*0*e^{3\pi+1} - e^{0*(3\pi + 1)} - ((0)*(1)*e^{1} - e^{(1*1)})$$ $$e - 1$$
Click to show solution for Question 5 5. What is the meaning of Curl and Divergence? Curl represents the tendency of the particles at that point to rotate around the direction of $curl\ \vec F$, and divergence is the amount a particle will move away from its location at that point. (Note: a conservative vector field is defined as a field where its curl is 0 and it has continuous second derivatives.) If so, find $f$ such that $\nabla f = \vec F$. $\vec F$ is conservative if $curl\ \vec F = 0$. So, let's find the curl: $$ curl\ \vec F = \begin{vmatrix} i && j && k \\ dx && dy && dz \\ P && Q && R \end{vmatrix} = (Rdy - Qdz)i + (Pdz - Rdx)j + (Qdx - Pdy)k $$ $$=\langle 0 - 0, 0 - 0, 6x - 3x\rangle$$ It is not conservative.

The work done is equal to the line integral: $$\int\limits_C \vec F \cdot dr$$ First, parameterize $C$. We can just define $0 \le t\le 1$ for simplicity. $$ C = \begin{cases} x = t \\ y = 2t + 1 \\ z = 2t + 2 \end{cases} $$ Then we use the formula for line integrals: $$\int\limits_C \vec F \cdot dr = \int\limits_a^b \vec F(\vec r(t)) \cdot \vec r'(t)$$ $$\int\limits_0^1 \langle (t)(2t + 2), (2t + 1)^2, 2(2t + 2)\rangle \cdot \langle 1,2,2 \rangle$$ $$\int\limits_0^1 10t^2 + 16t + 12 dt$$ $$\frac{10}{3}t^3 + 8t^2 + 12t |_0^1$$ $$\frac{10}{3} + 8 + 12$$
Click to show solution for Question 8 The surface area is the surface integral of 1. So: $$\iint\limits_S 1 dS = \iint\limits_D |r_u \times r_v|dA$$ First find the parameterization of $S$. This is simply the conversion to spherical coordinates: $$ S = \begin{cases} x = \rho\cos\theta\sin\phi \\ y = \rho\sin\theta\sin\phi \\ z = \rho\cos\phi \end{cases} $$ So, we need to find $r_u$ and $r_v$ $$r_u = \langle -\rho\sin\theta\sin\phi, \rho\cos\theta\sin\phi, 0\rangle$$ $$r_v = \langle \rho\cos\theta\cos\phi, \rho\sin\theta\cos\phi, -\rho\sin\phi \rangle$$ And find the cross product: $$ \begin{vmatrix} \hat i && \hat j && \hat k \\ -\rho\sin\theta\sin\phi && \rho\cos\theta\sin\phi && 0 \\ \rho\cos\theta\cos\phi && \rho\sin\theta\cos\phi && -\rho\sin\phi \end{vmatrix} $$ $$ = \langle -\rho^2\cos\theta\sin^2\phi, -\rho^2\sin\theta\sin^2\phi, -\rho^2\sin^2\theta\sin\phi\cos\phi - \rho^2\cos^2\theta\sin\phi\cos\phi \rangle $$ $$ = \langle -\rho^2\cos\theta\sin^2\phi, -\rho^2\sin\theta\sin^2\phi, -\rho^2\sin\phi\cos\phi \rangle $$ And find the length: $$\sqrt{\rho^4\cos^2\theta\sin^4\phi + \rho^4\sin^2\theta\sin^4\phi + \rho^4\sin^2\phi\cos^2\phi}$$ $$\sqrt{\rho^4\sin^4\phi + \rho^4\sin^2\phi\cos^2\phi}$$ $$\sqrt{\rho^4\sin^2\phi(\sin^2\phi + \cos^2\phi)}$$ $$\sqrt{\rho^4\sin^2\phi}$$ $$\rho^2\sin\phi$$ And take the integral: $$\int\limits_0^{2\pi}\int\limits_0^\pi \rho^2\sin\phi\ d\phi d\theta$$ $$2\pi * -\rho^2\cos\phi|_0^\pi$$ $$2\pi * -\rho^2((-1) - 1)$$ $$2\pi * 2\rho^2$$ $$4\pi\rho^2$$ Congratulations! You just derived the formula for the surface area of a sphere.
